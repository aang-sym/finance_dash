Reference · by impact
01
Eye area drives face attractiveness more than any other single feature
Predictions of full-face attractiveness based on the eye region alone outperform predictions based on any other facial feature. The brain evaluates a face within ~100ms and the eye area does most of the work.
[Yin et al., 2025]
02
White sclera is one of the strongest signals of health, youth, and attractiveness
Eyes with red or yellow sclera were rated as less healthy, less attractive, and older than identical eyes with white sclera. Sleep quality, alcohol reduction, and UV protection directly control this.
[Provine et al., 2013]
03
A darker limbal ring (iris outline) reads as younger and more attractive
The limbal ring thickens through youth and fades with age. Faces with a pronounced ring consistently won in forced-choice attractiveness tests against the same faces with a faded ring.
[Peshek et al., 2011]
04
Constricted pupils (more visible iris) are more attractive — the opposite of the common belief
Six experiments with ~3,000 participants found constricted pupils rated more attractive across the board. The effect held in black-and-white images — it is about iris brightness, not eye color.
[Cossu et al., 2024]
05
Lash length follows an inverted-U: optimal is ~1/3 of eye width
Attractiveness rises then falls with lash length. The peak is 1/3 of eye width. Most extensions push past 1/2 eye width — which is past the peak. For men, very long lashes reduce attractiveness more than no lashes at all.
[Pazhoohi & Kingstone, 2020] · [Pazhoohi & Kingstone, 2023]
06
Eyebrows carry as much perceptual weight as the eyes themselves
Face recognition dropped as much (or more) when brows were digitally removed vs. when eyes were removed. The brow frame directly changes how the eye underneath it reads.
[Sadr et al., 2003]
07
Side sleeping causes measurable eyelid asymmetry over years
People who consistently sleep on one side develop a measurably lower upper eyelid on that side. Lid position affects canthal tilt perception — the eye reads as more tired on the compressed side.
[Tran et al., 2022]
08
Retinoids applied to the orbital rim measurably reduce crow's feet
A 12-month split-face trial showed statistically significant reduction in crow's feet on the retinoid-treated side vs. untreated side. Apply to rim only — retinoids diffuse beneath skin and can irritate mucous membranes if placed too close.
[Li et al., 2021]
