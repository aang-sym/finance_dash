RHR is driven by cardiac efficiency (stroke volume). The more blood your heart pumps per beat, the less it needs to beat at rest. Zone 2 training is the primary intervention.

VO₂ Max measures how much oxygen your body can use at peak effort. It's trainable at any age but declines ~1%/year without intervention. Yours has fallen 30% since 2021. Two interventions are evidence-backed: Zone 2 volume (raises the floor) and short intervals (raise the ceiling).
THE TWO-INTERVENTION PROTOCOL
① Zone 2 base (3–4× / week) · PRIMARY
40–60 min at RPE 4–5. This builds mitochondrial density — the structural adaptation that lets your muscles extract more O₂. Must be truly easy. If you feel like you're working, you're too fast. Brisk walk, easy cycle, swim.
② 4×4 intervals (1× / week) · ACCELERATOR
After 4+ weeks of Z2 base: 4 sets × 4 min hard (RPE 8–9, HR ~145–155 bpm), 3 min easy between. This is the highest VO₂ max stimulus known. Norwegian 4×4 protocol — evidence: Helgerud et al. 2007.
PRACTICAL AMITRIPTYLINE NOTE
Amitriptyline blunts peak HR (chronotropic incompetence). Your true VO₂ max effort may feel harder at a lower HR than expected. Use RPE not HR as the guide for intervals. You may not hit 155 bpm even going very hard — that's the drug, not fitness.

HRV measures variability between heartbeats — higher = better autonomic (nervous system) recovery capacity. Your drop from 72ms (2021–23) to 35ms (2024–now) has two causes: amitriptyline's anticholinergic effect on the vagus nerve (hard to change) and fitness decline + lifestyle load (very changeable). Target 50–60ms is realistic on this medication.
WHAT ACTUALLY MOVES HRV
↑
Aerobic fitness (biggest lever)
The same Zone 2 training that lowers RHR also raises HRV by increasing vagal tone. 8–12 weeks of consistent training → +10–20ms.
↑
Slow breathing · 5–6 breaths/min
5 min/night before sleep. Inhale 4s, exhale 6s. Directly stimulates vagal tone. One of the only non-exercise interventions with strong RCT evidence. +5–15ms acutely.
↑
Sleep consistency
Same bedtime ±30 min is more important than total duration for HRV. Irregular sleep is a major suppressor.
↑
Cold exposure (optional)
30s cold shower finishing warm shower → acute vagal activation. Evidence is modest but mechanism is clear. Easy to try.
↓
Alcohol (acute)
2 drinks → −10–20ms next-morning HRV. Quantified in your Apple Watch data if you use the sleep HRV reading.
↓
Overtraining / high stress
If HRV drops 10%+ below your rolling baseline, reduce training intensity that day. Let the data guide recovery.
DAILY ROUTINE (5 min)
MORNING
HRV is measured by Apple Watch during the last phase of sleep — check it in Health app or here. If 10%+ below your 30-day avg, take it easy today. If normal or above, train as planned.
BEFORE SLEEP · 5 min breathing
Lie down, phone down. Breathe:
IN through nose · 4 seconds
OUT through mouth · 6 seconds
= 6 breaths/min · 5 min = 30 breaths

This is resonance frequency breathing — stimulates the baroreceptor reflex and vagal tone. You can use the free app "Breathwrk" or just count.
AMITRIPTYLINE CONTEXT
Anticholinergics block acetylcholine — the primary vagal neurotransmitter that drives HRV. This sets a ceiling. The interventions above work on the same pathway and can partially compensate, but you won't fully return to 72ms while on 100mg. At 75mg you'd likely gain +5–8ms from dose reduction alone.
Realistic HRV target on 100mg amitriptyline: 45–55ms within 6 months with consistent Z2 training + breathing practice + sleep regularity. At 75mg: 50–65ms.
Evidence basis
150 min/week moderate cardio target — WHO Global Action Plan for Physical Activity 2018; AHA guidelines. Moderate = Zone 2–3, RPE 4–6/10.
RHR reduction from aerobic training — meta-analysis of 191 studies (Reimers et al. 2018, J. Clin. Med.): endurance training reduces RHR ~5–8 bpm; effect larger when baseline is elevated.
Amitriptyline raises RHR +10–15 bpm — Brosen & Gram 1992 (Psychopharmacology): mean increase 78→94 bpm at 150mg/day.
Chronotropic incompetence on TCAs — JACC Case Reports 2022: cyclist lost 20 bpm off peak HR on desipramine; resolved on cessation.
QTc 453 ms (2024) — mildly abnormal for males (threshold 450 ms). Amitriptyline adds ~8–10 ms. Dangerous threshold: 500+ ms. Source: European Society of Cardiology guidelines.
Aerobic exercise and prediabetes — aerobic training is one of the most effective interventions for high-normal fasting glucose (ADA 2024 Standards of Care).
VO₂ Max 4×4 intervals — Helgerud et al. 2007 (Med. Sci. Sports Exerc.): 4×4 min at 90–95% HRmax × 3/week → +7.2 ml/kg/min VO₂ max in 8 weeks. Strongest VO₂ stimulus known.
Zone 2 for mitochondrial density — Maunder et al. 2021 (Sports Med.): low-intensity polarised training (80% Zone 2) produces superior mitochondrial adaptations vs moderate-intensity only.
Resonance frequency breathing for HRV — Lehrer & Gevirtz 2014 (Front. Psychol.): 5–6 breaths/min (4s in / 6s out) increases baroreflex sensitivity and HRV significantly; effect persists for hours post-session.
Amitriptyline anticholinergic HRV suppression — Gimenez-Maroto et al. 2020 (JACC): tricyclic antidepressants reduce HRV via vagal blockade; effect is dose-dependent and partially reversible.
