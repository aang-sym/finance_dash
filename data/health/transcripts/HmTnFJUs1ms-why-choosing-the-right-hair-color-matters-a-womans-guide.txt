Have you ever dyed your hair and felt disappointed by the result even though the color looked incredible on someone else? This is surprisingly common and there's actually a reason for it. So, what actually makes a hair color look good on someone? Most people think it's a shade itself, blonde, brunette, red. But thinking colleagues discovered something different when they digitally altered both hair color and skin color on women's faces and measured how these changes affected perceived age, health, and attractiveness. The result was surprising. The specific hair color mattered far less than the contrast between the hair and skin. Lower contrast, meaning hair color closer to your skin tone, resulted in a higher rating across all three dimensions. So, what does this mean practically, though? If you have fair, cool tone skin, ash blonde or cool brown tones will likely suit you better than jet black or warm copper. But if you have warm or darker skin, rich browns, warm chestnuts, or deep blacks will look the most harmonious. This also explains why your natural hair color, the one you were born with, usually looks best on you, because your genetics already optimize the pairing. Okay, but what about bleaching your hair in general? There's a widespread belief that blonde hair is more attractive. And there is some truth to this, but it's more nuanced than people think. Research by Sorowski revealed that blonde women were consistently rated as younger than women with darker hair. And yes, this makes biological sense. Women's hair naturally darkens with age, so lighter hair can function as a cue for youth. However, there is a critical point to note here. The attractiveness advantage of blonde hair only appeared for women in their late 20s and older. For younger women, there was no noticeable difference. So, if your natural hair color has darkened, strategic lightening can restore that youthfulness cue. But the key word here being strategic because even if you finally picked the optimal shade, the result can still look worse than before. Let us explain. and colleagues discovered that hair density had the strongest effect on attractiveness perception, stronger than both hair color and hairstyle. So, think about what this actually means. The thickness and fullness of your hair matters more than its color. And here's where hair coloring becomes problematic. Bleaching damages the protective outer shell of your hair, causing it to fragment and break apart, which is why bleached hair becomes so fragile, often resulting in that dry straw-like texture with increased frizz and a thinning appearance. But even regular dyeing isn't harmless. Permanent dyes use alkaline agents to open the cuticle and allow color to penetrate, which weakens the hair's protective barrier and strips away natural oils that give hair its shine. Even worse, according to Choy and colleagues, this damage is cumulative, meaning the harm builds up over time, getting noticeably worse after each treatment. So, you might theoretically achieve a better color, only to lose what really actually matters the most, the quality, the shine, the density.
