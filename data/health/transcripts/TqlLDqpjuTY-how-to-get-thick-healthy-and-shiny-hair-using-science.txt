You probably know the pain. You use expensive products, follow every trend, and you still end up with hair that looks thin and [music] damaged. Meanwhile, everyone on social media seems to have it figured out. The problem is that most hair care routines focus on products while ignoring [music] the actual causes of damage. So, today we're taking a different science-based approach. Step one is to wash your hair more often. This might contradict with what you've heard online. Common advice is to wash your hair only one or two times a week to avoid stripping natural oils. But, a recent study by Prinyani and colleagues surprisingly discovered the opposite to be true. The researchers conducted a control trial comparing different wash frequencies. Their findings were clear. Participants who shampooed five to six times per week reported the highest satisfaction with both scalp and hair condition. Those who washed daily scored better on every measured outcome than those who only washed once a week. The reasoning behind this is simple. When you wash infrequently, sebum, sweat, and dead skin cells accumulate on the scalp, creating an unfavorable environment. Your scalp is skin. We wash our face and body daily because of cleanliness supports healthy function. The same principle applies to your scalp. But, only when using a properly formulated shampoo, which leads us to step two. Check your shampoo's pH. Like the rest of your skin, your scalp has a pH of around 5.5. And here's where it gets problematic. Yavuz Onay DS and colleagues analyzed over 100 commercial shampoos and found pH levels ranging from 3.5 all the way to 9.0. Among popular brand shampoos, only 1/3 had a pH of 5.5 or below. This is concerning because research suggests that higher pH forces the cuticle open, which might lead to tangling, breakage, and frizz. That's why it might be worth looking for shampoos that specifically advertise pH balanced formulas. You can also buy simple pH strips and test your current products yourself. Step three. Protect your hair from SSC. It's a common knowledge that you need to protect your skin from the sun. Yet, most people completely forget about their hair. Sabatier and colleagues suggest that UVB radiation causes protein loss, while UVA causes color changes and pigment degradation. Both types of radiation seem to attack your hair's keratin structure. This might explain why your hair often feels drier and looks duller after your summer vacation. And unfortunately, salt water makes this even worse by additionally drawing moisture out through osmosis. Swimming pools pose a similar threat. According to Pires Oliveira and colleagues, the combination of pool water and sunlight is devastating. As stated in the study, the presence of chlorine in water associated with radiation was shown to be much more damaging to hair than each of these factors separately. You can counter this by using UV protection sprays if you spend significant time outdoors. Wear a hat during peak sun hours and always rinse your hair immediately after swimming in salt water or chlorinated pools. Step four, reduce mechanical damage. A significant portion of what appears as thin hair is just actually breakage. You might have plenty of density at the root, but if your strands are snapping off mid-length, they will never achieve a healthy look. The biggest culprits are habits people perform daily without a second thought, such as brushing wet hair, tight slick backs that create constant tension at the hairline, cotton pillow cases that generate friction throughout the night, and aggressive towel drying that roughs up the cuticle with every wash. The solutions are simple. First, use a wide-tooth comb only on dry or damp hair. Second, avoid tension-inducing hairstyles. Third, gently squeeze water out with a microfiber towel instead of rubbing. And fourth, optimally switch to a satin or silk pillow case. Mechanical damage is entirely within your control, and so is step five, avoid high heat damage. Heat styling is probably the single fastest way to destroy your hair's appearance. Research by Boris et al. identified 140° C as the critical threshold. Below this temperature, changes to the hair are mostly reversible. Above it, you start permanently breaking the bonds that hold the hair together. Most flat irons and curling irons operate between 150 and 230° C. That means almost every heat styling session is causing some level of permanent damage. And no, heat protectants don't help as much as you think. McMullen and Jack Fitz proved that they only reduce damage by about 20% at maximum. So, they're not a free pass to use high heat. The truth is, most people underestimate how much damage they've already caused and overestimate how healthy their hair actually is.
