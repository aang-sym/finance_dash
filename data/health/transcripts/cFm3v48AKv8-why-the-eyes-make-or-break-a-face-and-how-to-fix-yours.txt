TITLE: Why the Eyes Make or Break a Face (And How to Fix Yours)
VIDEO_ID: cFm3v48AKv8

Ever notice how someone's eyes are usually the first thing that you see and probably the last thing that you remember? Watch what happens when we alter only the eye area of these faces and leave something else untouched. Same jaw, same nose, same skin, and yet your impression of how attractive they look shifts significantly. Your brain evaluates a face within about a 100 milliseconds of seeing it and the eye area is doing most of that work. Research shows that predictions based on the eye region alone match full face attractiveness ratings better than predictions from just about any other feature. So, let's talk about what actually makes eyes attractive and how you can realistically improve yours. Look at the corner of your eyes. The angle between the inner corner and the outer corner is called the canthal tilt. If the outer corner sits higher than the inner, then your tilt is positive. If they sit level, then it's neutral. And if the outer sits lower, then it's negative. Researchers and guys tested how much this actually matters. They took female faces and subtly bumped up the positive tilt using image editing. In forced choice, raters preferred the altered version 93% of the time. Now, here's where most online discussions get it wrong. Canthal tilt is structural. It comes from the bony orbit that you were born with. No exercise is going to change it, no matter what social media is telling you. But the way people perceive your canthal tilt can absolutely shift. There's three things that you can try to shift it. First, the tail of your brow. If you lift the outer third of your brow, you visually extend the tilt. British model Oliver Cheshire is a great example of this. His long, high lateral brow adds a clear framing to the face and because the lateral brow descends with age, that upward tilt reads as youthful. Second, the placement of shadow or liner along the outer corner. Makeup artists have used this for decades for a reason. Third, and this one surprises a lot of people, your sleep position. Tran and colleagues found that people who consistently sleep on one side develop a measurably lower upper eyelid on that side over time. The lid drops, the tilt flattens asymmetrically, and the eye reads as more tired on the sleep side. None of these are bone level changes, but the eye reads noticeably different. Take a close look at any celebrity eye. Think of someone like Alexandra Daddario, Timothée Chalamet. The first thing you notice isn't the shape, it's the clarity. Provine and colleagues found that eyes with red or yellow sclera were rated as less healthy, less attractive, and older than the same eyes with untinted white. Eyes made wider than the natural baseline didn't become more attractive, but they did read as younger. The second layer is the iris. Peshek and colleagues tested faces with a darker limbal ring, which is the thin dark outline around the outside of the iris against the same face with the ring faded out. The darker ring version won in forced choice consistently. The ring thickens through youth and fades with age, which is probably why the brain registers it as an attractiveness cue. The third layer is the part that surprises almost everyone. For centuries, the assumption was that dilated pupils make eyes look more attractive. Renaissance women used to drop belladonna in their eyes to enlarge the pupils for exactly this reason. In 2024, Kosinski and colleagues ran six experiments with nearly 3,000 participants to finally settle the question. The opposite turned out to be true. Constricted pupils, which reveal more of the iris, were rated as more attractive across the board, and the effect held in black and white, which means it isn't about eye color. It's about brightness. More iris visible, brighter eye. So, what actually moves these three layers in your favor? Sleep and reducing alcohol both visibly drop the baseline redness. If you spend a lot of time on screens, lubricating drops do help, and over the long run, sunglasses and SPF really matter because UV is what slowly yellows the sclera. Lashes are where most people overshoot. The assumption is that longer is always better, but it's not. Basu and Kingston tested attractiveness across every lash length from none to half the width of the eye. The pattern was an inverted U shape. Attractiveness rose, peaked, and then fell. The peaks are at a lash to eye width ratio of roughly 1/3. A follow-up study replicated this across Asian, white, and Indian faces and raters, the 1/3 rule held across ethnicities with one small exception. For black women, the peak shifted slightly higher. Basu and Kingston's original work also tested male faces and found something quite interesting. For male faces, very long lashes actually reduce attractiveness more so than no lashes at all. So, why does this happen? Probably because lashes evolved to do a particular job. They reduce airflow and particle deposition on the eye, and they do that optimally at around 1/3 of eye width. Past that, they stop signaling function and start signaling something's off. So, measure the width of your eye, aim for lashes at roughly a third of that length. Most lash extensions and most falsies push past half, which is actually past the peak. But, if your natural lashes are short, serums like bimatoprost can add 20 to 25% length. For most people, that moves you closer to the ideal, but not past it. Model Jourdan Dunn, for example, reads striking without pushing past the peak. Your brows do way more work than you think. Sadr and colleagues ran a recognition experiment. They showed participants faces with either the eyes digitally removed or the brows removed. Face recognition dropped at least as much, and in some cases more, when the brows were missing than when the eyes were, which means the brows are carrying at least as much perceptual weight as the eye itself. And if the brow is wrong, it changes how the eye underneath it reads. Ideal brow position differs by sex. Women's brows sit slightly above the bony orbital rim, peaking lateral to the mid-pupil. Men's brows sit on the rim and stay flatter. There are three things that matter most for the brow as a frame. First is density. Fuller brows read as younger at any age. Overplucking, especially around the tail, ages the upper face faster than almost anything else. Thin brows can be thickened with minoxidil or bimatoprost applied precisely in a subset of users over roughly 16 weeks. The next thing is position. The lateral brow drops with age before the rest of because it sits past the reach of the frontalis muscle that lifts the rest of the brow. American actress Margaret Qualley is a useful reference here. Trimming the downturned portion of her brow noticeably freshened her look over the last few years. And finally, shape. Start the brow above your inner canthus, peak it between the lateral limbus and the outer corner, end it along the line running from your nasal ala through the outer corner. If you take care of just these three points, the effect on the overall face is much bigger than you would expect. The upper eye ages faster than almost any other part of your face. The skin around the eye is about 0.5 mm thick. Cheek skin, for comparison, is around 2.0. That makes it the thinnest skin on the face. And the orbicularis oculi, the muscle that closes your eye, contracts thousands of times a day pulling on that skin with every expression. Crow's feet show up first because of this reason. And the upper lid starts to hollow as the bony orbital rim loses projection gradually from your 30s onwards. Again, there are three things that really first topical retinoids. Li and colleagues ran a 12-month split-face trial and showed statistically significant reduction in crow's feet on the treated side compared to the untreated side of the same face. Apply retinoids along the orbital rim, not directly on the lid, and use the sandwich method, a layer of moisturizers before and after to limit general irritation. Second is daily SPF. UV drives both the collagen loss and the sclera yellowing. That's why sunglasses do more than people actually give them credit for. They shield the thinnest skin on your face from the exact wavelengths that tend to break it down. And finally, sleep. Back sleeping prevents the asymmetric lid descent we covered earlier. Side and stomach sleeping compress the same eye every night, and over years that side starts to look measurably older than the other. We have to keep in mind that every eye area is built from a different combination of these signals, and some matter much more for your specific face than others.
