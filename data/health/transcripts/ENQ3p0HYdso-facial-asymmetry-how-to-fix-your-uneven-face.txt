Lew and colleagues compared identical twins, same DNA, same starting point. Among many lifestyle differences, one stood out. The twins who regularly slept face down showed significantly greater nasal deviation and mouth asymmetry than their siblings. That makes sense. When you sleep face down or consistently on one side, soft tissue gets compressed for hours, night after night, for years on end. Even your eyelids are affected. Side sleepers tend to develop a measurably lower eyelid on their dominant sleep side. So, ideally, switch to back sleeping. If that's not an option, even just alternating sides regularly can have an impact. But you can't just reduce asymmetry through habits. You can also make it far less visible through how you present your face. Step two is redirecting the eye. Your bone structure might be fixed, but the way people perceive it isn't. And one of the most effective ways to influence this is through your hairstyle. Now, this is where it gets counterintuitive. If your face has noticeable asymmetry, your instinct would be to go for a clean, symmetrical hairstyle, a sharp middle part, or maybe a balanced cut. The assumption being that symmetrical hair will make your face look more symmetrical. But experienced hairstylists will tell you quite the opposite. A symmetrical style creates a visual reference line down the center of your face, and when your features don't match that reference, the unevenness becomes more noticeable, not less. That's why asymmetric styling tends to work better. A side part, messy texture, uneven volume distribution, these break up the visual field and prevent the eye from locking onto structural differences underneath. It's the same reason actors with subtle asymmetry, like Timothy Chalamet, Ryan Gosling, and Cara Hayward, consistently wear textured, irregular hair rather than clean, sanded styles. The third step is to balance your eye area. When you look at someone, the first place your gaze lands is almost always the eye area, which is exactly why asymmetry there is the hardest to hide. What most people don't realize is just how common this kind of asymmetry actually is. Hong and colleagues showed that up to 36% of people have measurable eye asymmetry, whether that shows up in a difference in their height, or their eye width, or their eye tilt. Most assume that there's nothing they can really do about it, short of surgery, but that's not quite true. Because your eyes are never perceived on their own, they're always read together with the brows above them, this is something that we can use to our advantage. If one of your eyes sit lower or looks smaller, you could shape the brows on that side slightly higher, with a slightly steeper arch. The eye underneath instantly looks more open, and the visual gap between your eyes shrinks, even though nothing about the eye itself has changed. The cleanest way to actually pull this off is to map each brow separately, instead of treating them as a pair. Find where each brow should start, arch, and end on its own side. Then trim and pluck towards those marks, instead of mirroring one side on the other. On the weaker side, leave the arch sitting a couple of millimeters higher than the other one. The other thing that could quietly do a lot of work for you is a pair of glasses. Prescription, sunglasses, fashion glasses, what they all share is a symmetrical frame that sits across the upper face like a built-in reference line. And when it shows it well, it pulls attention onto itself, and away from small differences underneath. Step four is to rebalance your jaw. At your next meal, pay attention to which side you instinctively start chewing on. If it's consistently the same, that might be part of the problem. Tuomainin and colleagues showed that 98% of people in their sample had a preferred chewing side. And interestingly, in that group, facial asymmetry tended to correspond with the preferred side, because the muscle responsible for chewing, called the masseter, gets loaded repeatedly on one side over months and years, that side develops more bulk and a more defined jaw angle. Think of it like training one arm more than the other. Eventually, the difference will show. A simple way to counter this is to start every meal on your weaker side. If you want to accelerate that process, you can also chew gum exclusively on the weaker side for 10 to 15 minutes per day. And if one side already feels noticeably larger or tighter, you could also massage it directly. Here our colleagues showed that targeted massage reduced masseter hardness and measurably decreased the right to left differences by helping to relax the overdeveloped muscle.
